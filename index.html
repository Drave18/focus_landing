02 Research

The science of the attention tax.

This isn’t a productivity theory. It’s a neurobiological fact. Most companies believe their teams are losing productivity due to poor execution or lack of time management. The science shows a completely different reality: your team’s cognitive architecture is being actively degraded by the modern digital workspace.

We aren’t failing at multitasking; we are succeeding at rapid, exhausting task-switching — and businesses are footing the bill.

  1. 01
    40% productivity drain

    The brain doesn’t multitask. It task-switches — and pays for every switch.

    Research from the American Psychological Association (Rubinstein, Meyer, and Evans) proves that the human brain cannot process parallel complex tasks. What we call “multitasking” is actually rapid context switching. This constant stopping and starting creates a severe cognitive load that costs up to 40% of a knowledge worker’s productive time every single day.

    American Psychological Association — Rubinstein, Meyer & Evans

  2. 02
    1,200 app switches per day

    Digital fragmentation costs five weeks of output per year.

    A Harvard Business Review study found that the average digital worker toggles between different applications and websites roughly 1,200 times per day — once every 24 seconds. This “digital fragmentation” requires the brain to constantly reorient, costing the average employee 5 full weeks of productive output per year just in transition time.

    Harvard Business Review

  3. 03
    40% performance drop

    Attention residue: your mind stays stuck on the last task.

    When an employee jumps from an unfinished email to a Zoom meeting, their brain doesn’t completely transition. Dr. Sophie Leroy’s landmark research defines this as attention residue. A portion of your cognitive capacity remains involuntarily stuck on the previous task. The result? A 30% to 40% drop in performance, higher error rates, and silent mental exhaustion on the current task.

    Dr. Sophie Leroy — University of Washington

  4. 04
    23:15 minutes to refocus

    One ping doesn’t cost five seconds. It costs a half-hour.

    It takes an average of 23 minutes and 15 seconds to fully regain deep cognitive focus after a single, minor interruption. When Slack pings or an unnecessary calendar alert pops up, you aren’t losing 5 seconds — you are losing a half-hour of peak intellectual capacity.

    Dr. Gloria Mark — UC Irvine

The conclusion

The problem is no longer time lost. It is the degradation of operational cognitive capacity — and the bill is paid in payroll every year.
